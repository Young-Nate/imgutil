Optimize Product Photos for Ecommerce: Compression, WebP/AVIF, Responsive Sizes, and Background Removal

Product photos are often the heaviest assets on an ecommerce page. And because shoppers scroll through many listings, the cost compounds: every extra megabyte increases time-to-first-view, hurts Core Web Vitals, and can reduce conversions. The good news is that most stores can cut product-image weight dramatically (often 40–90%) without making photos look worse—if you follow a few practical rules.

This guide gives you a repeatable workflow for optimizing product images in 2026: choose the right dimensions, compress efficiently, convert to modern formats (WebP/AVIF), serve responsive images, and use background removal where it helps. What “optimized product photos” actually means

For ecommerce, you’re usually optimizing for three things at the same time:

  • Fast loading: keep bytes low and avoid serving images larger than needed.
  • Sharp presentation: preserve edges, textures, and accurate color (especially for fabrics and cosmetics).
  • Consistency: uniform backgrounds and framing improve trust and make collections look more premium.

Optimization is less about a single “best” file format and more about a pipeline: resize → clean up → compress → convert → serve responsively.

Step 1: Start with the right dimensions (before you compress)

The most common ecommerce mistake is compressing a huge image instead of resizing it. If your product grid displays images at ~400 px wide, uploading a 3000 px wide JPEG is wasted bandwidth—even if you compress aggressively.

Recommended baseline sizes

  • Product grid / category cards: 320–600 px wide (serve multiple sizes via srcset).
  • Product detail page “main” image: 900–1400 px wide (depends on your zoom behavior).
  • Zoom / lightbox: 1600–2400 px wide (only if you truly support zooming).
Rule of thumb: set your largest served size to about 2× the maximum rendered size (for high-DPI displays). Anything bigger should be behind an explicit user action (zoom).

Step 2: Choose formats strategically (JPEG vs PNG vs WebP vs AVIF)

Format selection is where big savings happen, especially when you pair it with responsive delivery.

When to use each format

  • JPEG: still fine for photos when modern formats aren’t available, but usually heavier than WebP/AVIF for similar quality.
  • PNG: best when you truly need lossless quality or a hard alpha channel and your image has large flat areas (logos, UI). For photos, PNG is typically too heavy.
  • WebP: great general-purpose choice; strong compression; widely supported; good balance for product photos.
  • AVIF: often the smallest at similar quality, especially for high-detail photos—but encoding can be slower, so batch workflows matter.

If you want a deeper format comparison, see: AVIF vs WebP in 2026.

Practical guidance for ecommerce

  • Default: serve AVIF when supported, fall back to WebP, and then JPEG/PNG as a last fallback.
  • Keep masters: store a high-quality “master” (often PNG or high-quality JPEG) for future re-exports.
  • Test textures: fabrics, hair, and shiny objects reveal artifacts earlier—don’t over-compress those categories.

Step 3: Compress product images without visible quality loss

Compression is where you trade bytes for quality. For ecommerce photos, you typically want “visually lossless”: smaller files with no obvious artifacts at normal viewing sizes.

What to look for when you compress

  • Banding in smooth gradients (backgrounds, studio lighting).
  • Ringing around high-contrast edges (e.g., product silhouette against white).
  • Mushy textures (fabric weave, wood grain, labels).
Compression checklist (fast):
  1. Resize to your target max width first.
  2. Export to WebP or AVIF for the web version.
  3. Zoom to 100% and inspect edges + textures.
  4. If it still looks clean, push quality down one step and repeat until you notice damage—then step back up.

Step 4: Use background removal to increase consistency (and reduce bytes)

Background removal isn’t only an aesthetic choice. A clean, consistent background can:

  • Make your product grid feel cohesive (especially across multiple suppliers).
  • Reduce visual noise so compression artifacts are less noticeable.
  • Enable smaller crops because the subject is clearer.

When background removal is a good idea

  • Marketplace-style catalogs with mixed lighting and inconsistent backdrops.
  • Apparel photos where you want a uniform white or transparent background.
  • Product stickers / decals / items that look better floating on a neutral background.

Step 5: Convert in batches (WebP/AVIF) for real workflows

Ecommerce teams rarely optimize a single image. You’ll typically be handling dozens or hundreds of SKUs, seasonal refreshes, and new colorways.

A simple batching approach

  1. Create a folder of resized “web masters” (your target max width).
  2. Convert that set to AVIF and/or WebP.
  3. Spot-check 10–20 images across categories for artifacts.
  4. Keep the same settings for the rest, unless a category needs special treatment.

Step 6: Serve responsive images (srcset) so you stop over-downloading

Even if you export a modern format, you can still waste bandwidth by serving one “large” file to every device. Responsive images let browsers pick the smallest acceptable resource for the user’s screen.

Example: a practical ecommerce image tag

Here’s a simplified pattern using AVIF/WebP with fallbacks:

<picture>
  <source type="image/avif"
          srcset="/img/product-400.avif 400w,
                  /img/product-800.avif 800w,
                  /img/product-1200.avif 1200w"
          sizes="(max-width: 600px) 50vw, 300px" />
  <source type="image/webp"
          srcset="/img/product-400.webp 400w,
                  /img/product-800.webp 800w,
                  /img/product-1200.webp 1200w"
          sizes="(max-width: 600px) 50vw, 300px" />
  <img src="/img/product-800.jpg" width="800" height="800" alt="Product name" loading="lazy" />
</picture>

If you want the full responsive-images playbook, including sizes strategies and performance gotchas, see: Responsive images: srcset + WebP/AVIF.

Tip: Always include width and height to reduce layout shift (CLS). And use loading="lazy" for below-the-fold images.

Step 7: Upscale only when you must (and do it before you export)

If your product images are too small (common with old catalogs or supplier feeds), you might need to upscale. Upscaling won’t create true detail, but a good AI upscaler can improve perceived sharpness and reduce the “blocky” look of low-res originals.

  • Upscale first, then resize to the target max width.
  • Then compress and convert to your web formats.
  • Don’t upscale everything: reserve it for hero images or key SKUs.

Quick “do this today” checklist

  • Resize: create 400/800/1200 px variants for each product photo.
  • Convert: export AVIF + WebP; keep JPEG fallback if needed.
  • Compress: tune quality by category (textured items need higher quality).
  • Background: remove or standardize if your catalog looks inconsistent.
  • Deliver: implement <picture> + srcset + sizes on grid and PDP.
  • Verify: check a few pages on mobile over cellular and compare load time.

Conclusion

Optimizing product photos is one of the highest-leverage performance upgrades you can make for ecommerce. With the right dimensions, modern formats (WebP/AVIF), responsive delivery, and sensible compression, you can make pages feel dramatically faster without sacrificing visual quality.
