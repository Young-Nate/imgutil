AVIF vs WebP in 2026: which image format should you use for faster websites?

Updated April 11, 2026 • ~7–9 min read

AVIF and WebP both exist for the same reason: JPEG and PNG are often bigger than they need to be for modern websites. If you publish product photos, blog images, screenshots, or social graphics, switching formats can cut image weight dramatically, which improves page speed, Core Web Vitals, and the overall “snappiness” of your site.

This guide compares AVIF vs WebP in practical terms: what each format is good at, what can go wrong, and a simple workflow to choose the right format for each image type. At the end, you’ll also see how to convert and optimize images locally in your browser using Pixl’s free tools—no uploads required.

Quick takeaway (if you’re in a hurry)

  • Use AVIF for most photographic images when you want the smallest file sizes and can accept a bit more encoding complexity.
  • Use WebP when you need a safe default with great quality, wide support, and strong results for photos and simple graphics.
  • Keep PNG for edge cases like pixel-perfect UI assets, line art that doesn’t compress well, or when you need maximum compatibility for downloads.

Real-world performance depends on your encoder settings and the type of image. Don’t guess—test a few representative images from your site.

What are AVIF and WebP (in plain English)?

WebP: a modern replacement for JPEG and PNG

WebP is an image format designed for the web. It supports both lossy and lossless compression, plus transparency (alpha) and animation. It typically produces smaller files than JPEG/PNG at similar visual quality, especially for web photos.

AVIF: next-generation compression (often even smaller)

AVIF is based on AV1 video compression technology adapted for still images. In many cases, AVIF can reach noticeably smaller file sizes than WebP at comparable quality—particularly for detailed photos and gradients. The tradeoff is that encoding can be slower, and compatibility or tooling may vary depending on your workflow.

AVIF vs WebP comparison: the factors that actually matter

1) File size and quality

If your main goal is smallest possible files for photo-heavy pages, AVIF is often the winner. WebP is still excellent and frequently “good enough,” especially if you already compress aggressively. In practice, the best approach is to try both formats on your typical images and choose what meets your quality bar at the lowest size.

2) Browser support and compatibility

By 2026, both formats are broadly supported in modern browsers. The most common compatibility problems happen not in browsers, but in older CMS plugins, email clients, design tools, or asset pipelines that assume JPEG/PNG. If your images appear in places beyond a modern web page (downloads, email newsletters, legacy apps), WebP may be the safer “modern default,” with JPEG/PNG fallbacks where necessary.

3) Transparency (alpha)

Both AVIF and WebP support transparency. For transparent UI elements, stickers, or cut-outs, you can usually replace PNG with WebP or AVIF and save a lot of weight. The key is to check edges: halos around cut-outs or banding in soft shadows can show up if you compress too hard.

4) Animation

Animated WebP is a common alternative to GIF and often results in much smaller files. AVIF also supports animation, but tooling and production pipelines are still more frequently built around WebP. If animation is core to your workflow, test carefully and consider sticking with animated WebP unless you have a strong reason to move.

5) Encoding speed and workflow friction

WebP is typically easier to produce quickly in many tools. AVIF can be slower to encode, and some settings are less intuitive. For large batch jobs, this matters. For a small blog, it may not. Your best format is the one you can reliably generate and ship.

When to use AVIF (best use cases)

  • Hero images and large photos where every KB matters.
  • Photography with gradients (sky, studio backgrounds) where AVIF can keep quality high at lower sizes.
  • High-DPI product photos where you want crisp detail while staying fast.

Practical tip: Start by converting 10–20 of your highest-traffic images to AVIF and measure page speed and visual quality. If the workflow is painless and the savings are real, expand from there.

When to use WebP (best use cases)

  • General-purpose site images when you want great compression with minimal surprises.
  • Images with transparency that replace PNG assets.
  • Animated content when you want a smaller alternative to GIF.
  • Teams and clients where a format that “just works” across tooling matters more than squeezing the last 5–15%.

A simple decision workflow (choose AVIF vs WebP in under a minute)

Step 1: classify the image

  • Photo / realistic image: test AVIF first, then WebP if AVIF creates artifacts or you need easier handling.
  • UI screenshot / mixed content: start with WebP; if text looks soft, reduce compression or consider PNG for that asset.
  • Logo / flat color / line art: try lossless WebP; if sizes are not great, PNG may still win.
  • Transparent cut-out: try WebP or AVIF; check edge quality.

Step 2: pick a quality target instead of a format target

A good optimization workflow prioritizes consistent visual quality. Choose a target like “looks identical at normal viewing” or “minor differences allowed” and compress until you hit the smallest file that meets that bar.

Step 3: ship with fallbacks if your stack needs them

If you’re using HTML <picture>, you can offer AVIF first, then WebP, then JPEG as a last resort. Many modern frameworks and CDNs can generate this automatically. If you can’t, using WebP alone is still a huge win.

Common mistakes that make AVIF/WebP look bad

Over-compressing textured areas

Hair, fabric, foliage, and detailed surfaces can look “crunchy” if compression is too aggressive. If you see speckling or smearing, increase quality a bit or try the other format.

Ignoring color banding in gradients

Gradients (sky, studio backdrops, shadows) can show banding at low bitrates. AVIF often handles gradients well, but settings matter. Always check your largest gradients at 100% zoom.

Making text unreadable in screenshots

Screenshots can be tricky: they contain sharp edges and fine text. WebP usually does well, but if text looks soft, reduce compression, consider lossless WebP, or keep PNG for that image.

FAQ: AVIF vs WebP

Should I use AVIF everywhere?

Not necessarily. AVIF can be a great default for large photos, but WebP is still an excellent all-around format. Use whichever meets your quality target with the smallest size and least workflow friction.

Is WebP still worth it if AVIF is smaller?

Yes. WebP often gets you most of the win with simpler tooling. For many sites, converting JPEG/PNG to WebP is already a big performance upgrade.

Do I need both formats?

If your stack supports <picture> sources or automatic image generation, offering AVIF + WebP is ideal. If not, pick one (WebP is a safe choice) and focus on proper compression and sizing.
