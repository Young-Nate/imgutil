If your site feels slow, images are often the easiest win. The good news: you don’t need Photoshop, a desktop app, or a cloud upload flow to modernize your image formats. With the right approach, you can convert an entire folder of JPG/PNG images to WebP and AVIF in one pass, keep quality high, and cut page weight dramatically.

This guide walks through when to use WebP vs AVIF, how to pick sensible quality settings, and a practical batch workflow using Pixl’s batch converter (runs locally in your browser, so your files never leave your device).

Why batch conversion matters (and when it’s worth doing)

Batch converting makes the most sense when you have:

  • Many legacy images (old blog posts, product galleries, documentation screenshots).
  • Repeated usage (the same image used across multiple pages or locales).
  • Performance goals (Core Web Vitals, mobile-first speed, better LCP).

Even a “small” average saving of 40–70 KB per image becomes huge across a site with hundreds or thousands of assets. When you combine modern formats with good sizing and caching, the effect is usually visible in real-world load times.

WebP vs AVIF: which format should you generate?

Choose WebP when…

  • You want excellent compatibility across modern browsers.
  • You need a strong “default” for photos and many graphics.
  • You prefer faster encode times in many workflows.

Choose AVIF when…

  • You’re optimizing photo-heavy pages and want maximum compression.
  • You can accept slower encoding in exchange for smaller files.
  • You’re already using a fallback strategy (e.g., AVIF → WebP → JPG/PNG).

In practice, many teams generate both: AVIF for browsers that support it and WebP as the fallback. If you want a deeper comparison, see: AVIF vs WebP (2026).

The three levers that control image weight

When you convert images, file size is mostly driven by these choices:

1) Dimensions (the biggest lever)

Serving a 2400px-wide hero image into a 900px container wastes bytes. Before you batch convert, decide your target widths. For most sites, a sensible baseline might be:

  • Thumbnails: 200–400px wide
  • Inline content: 900–1200px wide
  • Full-bleed heroes: 1600–2400px wide (depending on layout)

If you can resize and then convert, you often get a bigger improvement than format conversion alone.

2) Quality setting (find the “good enough” point)

Quality is not linear. Dropping from 95 → 85 often saves little; 80 → 65 might save a lot; 65 → 45 can start to look rough. A practical starting point for photos:

  • WebP: try quality 75–82
  • AVIF: try quality 45–60 (AVIF typically looks good at lower values)

Then test on a few representative images (faces, gradients, product shots, text overlays). Keep a small set of “golden” test images to compare before you run a giant batch.

3) Content type (photo vs UI vs logos)

Photos compress well with modern lossy formats. UI screenshots with crisp text and sharp edges are trickier. For those, use a slightly higher quality, or consider a lossless WebP when you truly need pixel-perfect text. If your screenshot is already tiny, converting formats may not help much—focus on cropping and resizing first.

A practical batch workflow (no installs, no uploads)

Here’s a simple workflow you can repeat whenever you ship new content:

Step 1: Audit your current assets

  • Sort by size and identify the top 20 heaviest images.
  • Check whether they’re oversized for their on-page display.
  • Decide which images should get AVIF + WebP, and which can stay as-is.

Step 2: Convert a small test batch first

Grab a handful of images from different categories (hero photos, product images, blog inline images, UI screenshots). Convert them, then compare:

  • Visual quality at 100% zoom
  • Banding in gradients
  • Artifacts around text or logos

Step 3: Run the full batch conversion

  • Keep names predictable: same filename, new extension makes deploys easier.
  • Separate outputs: export AVIF and WebP into separate folders if your pipeline needs it.
  • Spot check: open a few outputs from different parts of the batch before you commit them.

How to serve AVIF/WebP on the web (with fallbacks)

Once you’ve converted, shipping the right format to the right browser is the next step. The most common approach is the <picture> element:

<picture>
  <source srcset="/images/hero.avif" type="image/avif">
  <source srcset="/images/hero.webp" type="image/webp">
  <img src="/images/hero.jpg" alt="Your description" width="1200" height="800" loading="lazy">
</picture>

This lets supporting browsers take AVIF, otherwise WebP, otherwise your original JPG/PNG fallback. Always include width and height (or equivalent CSS sizing) to reduce layout shift.

Common mistakes to avoid

Converting everything to AVIF without testing

AVIF is great, but some images (especially sharp-edged UI) can show artifacts at aggressive settings. Start with a mixed set, validate quality, then batch.

Ignoring resize and shipping giant dimensions

Format conversion helps, but oversized dimensions still cost. If your CMS or build pipeline supports responsive images, generate multiple sizes and use srcset.

Forgetting that compression stacks

If you compress a JPG and then convert to WebP/AVIF, you might carry over earlier artifacts. For best results, convert from the highest-quality source available.
